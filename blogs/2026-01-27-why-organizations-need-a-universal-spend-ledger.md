<p>Everyone agrees technology spend is out of control. An enormous risk and opportunity for businesses today. Between cloud, SaaS, data center, AI, internal platforms, etc. technology is now one of the fastest-growing lines on the P&amp;L. And yet most companies are still trying to manage it with tools and processes that were never designed for how technology is actually consumed today.</p>



<p>That’s why <a href="https://ternary.app/blog/what-is-finops/">FinOps</a> is evolving beyond the cloud to encompass all technology spend.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Here’s the blunt reality: <strong>You cannot manage technology spend strategically if you don’t have a single system of record for it.</strong> </p>



<p>Most companies don’t.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-where-technology-spend-actually-breaks-down">Where technology spend actually breaks down</h2>



<p>In almost every organization I talk to, the same pattern shows up:</p>


<div class="wp-block-list-wrapper">
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Finance owns the budget</li>



<li>Engineering controls the spend</li>



<li>Executives are accountable for outcomes</li>
</ul>
</div>


<p>But the data that connects those three things is fragmented. Cloud costs live in one place. SaaS contracts live somewhere else. Revenue data lives in finance systems. Usage data lives with engineering. Headcount and internal effort live nowhere useful.</p>



<p>Finance is asked to forecast spend it doesn’t control. Engineering is asked to optimize costs it doesn’t fully see. Executives are asked to make decisions without a clear link between spend and results.</p>



<p>This isn’t a people problem. It’s a data model problem.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-a-universal-spend-ledger-tm-actually-enables">What a Universal Spend Ledger<sup>TM</sup> actually enables</h2>



<p><a href="https://ternary.app/blog/what-is-technology-spend-management/">Technology Spend Management</a> only becomes real when <strong>all technology spend and business context live in the same analytical plane</strong>. That’s what a Universal Spend Ledger<sup>TM</sup> enables.</p>



<p>Here are concrete examples of what organizations start doing once this exists.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">1. Real margin and unit cost modeling</h3>



<p>Instead of guessing, organizations can answer questions like:</p>


<div class="wp-block-list-wrapper">
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>What does it actually cost us to serve a customer?</li>



<li>What’s the fully-loaded cost of a product, environment, or region?</li>



<li>How does margin change as usage scales?</li>
</ul>
</div>


<p>This requires joining:</p>


<div class="wp-block-list-wrapper">
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Cloud and infrastructure spend</li>



<li>SaaS and tooling costs</li>



<li>Internal platform costs</li>



<li>Revenue or business data</li>
</ul>
</div>


<p>Without a unified ledger, this analysis is slow, manual, and usually abandoned halfway through. With it, margins become a living metric and not a quarterly exercise.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">2. Finance-led guardrails without blocking engineering</h3>



<p>Most FinOps tools stop at giving engineers visibility into spend. That visibility is necessary, but it is not sufficient on its own.</p>



<p>Technology Spend Management goes a step further by elevating finance as the primary owner of financial guardrails and priorities, while still enabling engineering to operate independently within clear boundaries. Rather than telling engineers what to do, finance defines the constraints that matter most to the business.</p>



<p>In practice, this looks like:</p>


<div class="wp-block-list-wrapper">
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Finance sets unit cost targets for a service</li>



<li>Engineering sees those targets alongside usage and architectural decisions</li>



<li>Tradeoffs become explicit and data-driven, rather than emotional</li>
</ul>
</div>


<p>This model only works when finance and engineering are working from the same numbers, derived from the same underlying system of record.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">3. Evaluating AI spend like a business investment</h3>



<p><a href="https://ternary.app/blog/ai-spend/">AI spend</a> is breaking every existing model.</p>



<p>Usage-based pricing, opaque vendor metrics, and experimental workloads make it nearly impossible to answer basic questions:</p>


<div class="wp-block-list-wrapper">
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Is this AI workload improving outcomes?</li>



<li>What’s the cost per inference, per user, per dollar of revenue?</li>



<li>Which experiments should we scale and which should we kill?</li>
</ul>
</div>


<p>A Universal Spend Ledger<sup>TM</sup> brings AI usage, infrastructure costs, and business impact together for analysis. Without it, AI spend becomes an act of faith.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">4. From cost cutting to capital allocation</h3>



<p>The biggest shift Technology Spend Management enables is psychological. When all technology spend is visible, normalized, and tied to outcomes, the conversation changes from:</p>



<p>“Where can we cut?” to “Where should we invest more?”</p>



<p>That’s the difference between cost management and capital allocation. And it’s the difference between reacting to spend and using it strategically.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Why existing tools can’t get you there</h2>



<p>ERP systems were not built for usage-based pricing. SaaS management tools focus narrowly on licenses. Spreadsheets do not scale. And many <a href="https://ternary.app/blog/cloud-cost-management-tools/">FinOps tools</a> were built cloud-first and engineering-first, rather than with the business in mind.</p>



<p>The core failure is structural. No system was designed to serve as the system of record for all technology spend.</p>



<p>Effective Technology Spend Management requires one.</p>
